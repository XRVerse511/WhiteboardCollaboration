Bare-Hand Interaction for Whiteboard Collaboration

Author: Guangtian Liu
Beijing University of Posts and Telecommunications

Introduction

Today, VR systems relying on device-based interactions, such as controllers or gloves, often face significant usability challenges. These devices can feel unnatural, requiring users to learn complex control schemes that disrupt immersion. Moreover, the reliance on hardware introduces logistical issues like cost, weight, and battery life, creating barriers for widespread adoption. For users, these devices may also lead to fatigue during prolonged sessions and restrict accessibility in scenarios where quick setup or minimal equipment is desirable. Such limitations hinder the vision of seamless, intuitive VR experiences and restrict VR's applicability across diverse fields such as education, healthcare, and entertainment.

Instead of relying on physical devices, we propose leveraging the user's natural hand movements as the primary interaction mechanism—an approach enabled by advances in hand-tracking technologies and machine learning for gesture recognition. This shift eliminates the dependency on external hardware, providing a more intuitive and immersive experience. By using cameras and sensors to accurately track hand positions and interpret gestures, VR systems can replicate real-world interactions in the virtual space, offering users the freedom to grab, push, or manipulate virtual objects without breaking immersion.

Our research focuses on developing scalable hand-tracking and reconstruction solutions capable of maintaining high precision and low latency across a wide range of environments. Specifically, we are improving gesture recognition and reconstruction algorithms to handle occlusion, dynamic lighting, and complex backgrounds while ensuring efficient real-time processing. This enables interactions that feel fluid and responsive, even in challenging conditions. By integrating haptic feedback mechanisms and multi-modal interaction systems, such as combining hand gestures with voice commands or eye tracking, we further aim to enhance user control and engagement.

Our recent research has achieved notable advancements in VR bare-hand interaction, particularly in whiteboard collaboration within virtual environments. We addressed challenges in recognizing pen-drop, writing, and pen-lift intentions, proposing two methods: Air-writing, which adjusts the virtual whiteboard's position for consistent writing without physical surfaces, and Physical-writing, which uses passive haptic feedback by aligning the virtual whiteboard with real-world planes. User studies demonstrated an 8% improvement in communication efficiency over controllers, with Physical-writing achieving higher accuracy and user satisfaction. These results highlight the potential of bare-hand interaction to deliver immersive, natural, and efficient VR experiences.
